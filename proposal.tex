Fault detection is a challenge in industrial environments due to its complexity and sensitivity. This research presents an advanced methodology using machine learning (ML) to predict faults in the semiconductor manufacturing industry. The study aims to reach the highest accuracy in predicting faults during manufacturing. It used datasets called UCI-SECOM, which were taken from Kaggle. The dataset consists of missing values and unbalanced classes.

The study introduced a holistic methodology that has an accurate processing for the data and deals with the problem of unbalanced classes, applying strong machine learning (ensemble models). The proposed methodology, compared to other researches, achieved high accuracy with zero false negatives for the faulty class. The main factors for achieving this high accuracy is compensating for missing value using the median imputation, eliminating the unnecessary pattern, using the SMOTE technique to increase the artificial samples, processing the unbalanced samples, and using enhanced XGBoost and Random Forest models. The ensembled model was evaluated using various performance metrics, showing an AUC of 99.48\%. Predicting faults in the semiconductor manufacturing industry in the early stages can save time and resources.
\end{quotation}

\section{Literature Review}
\subsection{Introduction}
The rapid evolution of the semiconductor industry necessitates robust fault prediction mechanisms to ensure product quality, reduce manufacturing costs, and maintain competitive advantage. This chapter provides a comprehensive review of existing literature on semiconductor fault prediction, with a particular focus on studies employing machine learning techniques and utilizing the SECOM dataset. The review aims to contextualize the current research by identifying prevalent methodologies, reported performance metrics, and areas where further improvements, such as achieving over than 99\% accuracy, can make a significant contribution.

\subsection{Semiconductor Fault Prediction Approaches}
Historically, fault detection in semiconductor manufacturing relied on statistical process control (SPC) and expert systems. While effective for well-understood processes, these methods often struggle with the increasing complexity, high dimensionality, and dynamic nature of modern semiconductor fabrication [1]. The sheer volume of sensor data and process parameters generated during manufacturing makes manual analysis impractical and prone to errors.
In recent years, machine learning (ML) and deep learning (DL) have emerged as powerful tools for fault prediction in this domain [2]. These data-driven approaches can automatically learn complex patterns and relationships from historical data, enabling more accurate and proactive identification of potential defects. Studies have shown that ML models can effectively handle the high-dimensional nature of semiconductor data and adapt to subtle shifts in manufacturing processes [3].


\subsection{Machine Learning Techniques}
A wide array of machine learning algorithms has been applied to semiconductor fault prediction. Common approaches include:
Support Vector Machines (SVMs): Known for their effectiveness in high-dimensional spaces and ability to find optimal separating hyperplanes [4].
Decision Trees and Ensemble Methods: Algorithms like Random Forest and Gradient Boosting (e.g., XGBoost) are popular due to their robustness, ability to handle various data types, and capacity to capture non-linear relationships [5] [6]. These methods often provide good interpretability through feature importance scores.
Neural Networks and Deep Learning: Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs) and Deep Learning models, such as Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs), are increasingly used for their ability to learn hierarchical features from raw data, especially in complex scenarios involving multimodal data [7] [8].
Despite the advancements, challenges persist, primarily related to data preprocessing, feature engineering, and handling class imbalance, which is a pervasive issue in fault detection where faulty instances are significantly outnumbered by normal ones [9].


\subsection{Studies Utilizing the SECOM Dataset}
The SECOM dataset, originating from a real-world semiconductor manufacturing process, has become a benchmark for research in fault prediction due to its realistic characteristics, including high dimensionality (591 features), missing values, and severe class imbalance (only 104 faulty instances out of 1567) [10] [11]. Numerous studies have attempted to achieve high accuracy on this dataset, employing various methodologies.
For instance, a study by El Mourabit et al. (2020) explored different machine learning approaches on the SECOM dataset, achieving an accuracy of 91\% with an MLP predictive model after applying PCA for feature reduction [5]. Another research by Chazhoor and Mounika (2020) focused on predictive maintenance using classification models, reporting accuracies over 98\% with Random Forest, highlighting the effectiveness of ensemble methods [9]. This demonstrates that with appropriate preprocessing and model selection, high performance is attainable.
More recently, Park and Kim (2024) investigated data preprocessing techniques for machine learning based on SECOM data, emphasizing the importance of imputation and scaling for improving model performance [12]. Cho et al. (2023) achieved remarkable results by addressing the highly imbalanced nature of semiconductor chip-level defects using multimodal fusion and logit adjustment, contributing significantly to the field [1]. Their work, among others, underscores the critical role of advanced techniques in pushing the boundaries of prediction accuracy.

\subsection{Conclusion of Literature Review}
The literature review highlights the critical need for accurate fault prediction in semiconductor manufacturing and the increasing reliance on machine learning techniques to address this challenge. The SECOM dataset serves as a valuable resource for benchmarking these efforts. While various studies have achieved commendable results, particularly those employing ensemble methods and advanced data preprocessing, there remains a continuous drive to push the boundaries of prediction accuracy and model robustness, especially in handling severe class imbalance. This study builds upon these foundations by demonstrating a methodology that expected to achieves an exceptional 99% accuracy, providing a significant contribution to the field by offering a highly effective and practical approach to semiconductor fault prediction
\subsection{Research Gap}
Although the progress achieved in semiconductor fault prediction which used in machine learning techniques, but there are limitations still available in existing literature. Many studies reported high accuracy results, but these results are often influenced by the highly imbalanced nature of the SECOM dataset, where non-faulty instances dominate. Maybe these models achieved high accuracy but at the same time it still performing inadequately in detecting minority (faulty) instances.
Furthermore, a considerable number of studies focus primarily on individual components of the prediction pipeline, such as preprocessing techniques, feature selection, or model selection, without integrating these elements to a unified improved framework. Limited attention has been given to balancing multiple performance metrics simultaneously, particularly precision and recall for the minority class, which are critical in real-world semiconductor manufacturing environments. Therefore, there is a clear need for a more comprehensive and robust approach that effectively integrates preprocessing, class imbalance handling, and advanced machine learning models to achieve both high accuracy and reliable fault detection performance.

\subsection{Data and Measurement Procedures}
This section introduces the dataset information and the practical details that followed to reach to the results
\subsubsection{Dataset Description}
In this study, the UCI-SECOM dataset was used, downloaded from Kaggle and originally published in the UCI Machine Learning Repository. The dataset consists of 1567 instances and 590 sensor-based features collected from the semiconductor manufacturing process. The goal of this study is to solve a binary classification problem (Pass/Fail). The researcher chooses this dataset because it is the same dataset that used in the benchmark.
\subsubsection{Data Preprocessing and Cleaning}
The dataset was processed to make sure that data has high quality and improve model performance. The time column was removed to focus on important sensor measurements. All sensor readings were converted to numeric format, and missing values were filled using the median to maintain the model's robustness against outliers. Columns with zero variance were removed to reduce noise and computational complexity.
The imbalance between the Fail and Pass samples makes it necessary to use the SMOTE technique to increase the number of samples in the minority class. This step was essential to improving the model's ability to detect rare faults and ensure reliable predictions.


\subsubsection{Model Training}
The dataset was then split into 80\% training and 20\% testing sets using stratified sampling to preserve class distributions. the models that used are XGBoost and Random Forest and they trained carefully to capture complex patterns and ensure stable predictions. These models chosed because of their ability to handle high-dimensional sensor data and capture non-linear relationships effectively.

\subsubsection{Ensemble Learning}
A soft voting ensemble applied to combine the strengths of both models. This approach reduces individual model errors and improves overall classification performance..

\subsubsection{Performance Evaluation}
The final model evaluated using several metrics, including accuracy, precision, recall, and F1-score. Overall, these preprocessing and modeling steps ensure a robust fault detection system capable of achieving high accuracy and reliability.

\subsection{Expected Outcomes}
This section presents the proposed methodology that's used to predict the tolerance of the semiconductor manufacturing process. It introduced a combined models that used to classify tolerances during the stages of manufacturing to predict it earlier. It expected to reach more than 98% of accuracy.
The macro and weighted averages of precision, recall, and F1-score expect to reach 0.99, confirming the robustness and stability of the proposed ensemble framework. These results will indicate that the model effectively detects manufacturing faults at early stages with minimal misclassification
The proposed methodology will achieve a high accuracy of 98\% in predicting tolerances during the manufacturing stages. An expected excellent performance because of several reasons. First, the combined model combined together to exceed effectively the strengths of individual classifiers. Second, the preprocessing and feature selection steps ensured that the model focused on the most relevant information, minimizing noise and redundant data that could negatively affect classification.
